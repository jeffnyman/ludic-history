The history of computing presents an intriguing landscape, offering a plethora of theoretical and historiographical frameworks that can be effectively employed. This inherent diversity adds a layer of fascination to the subject, as it is intricately interwoven with key historical developments spanning the late 19th and 20th centuries, encompassing technical, social, economic, and political facets. In essence, it stands as a subject of paramount significance not only for specialists in the history of technology but also for a myriad of scholars across diverse disciplines.

The richness of the history of computing extends its relevance to social and cultural historians, economists, business historians, management theorists, labor historians, sociologists of professions, organizational theorists, and computer scientists alike. This breadth underscores its role as a focal point for interdisciplinary exploration, drawing scholars from various fields to unravel the multifaceted tapestry of its evolution and impact on the broader historical landscape.

Recent scholarship in the history of technology has unveiled a profound and enduring insight: technological change is not solely propelled by inherent technological imperatives but is equally shaped by intricate social processes. This realization challenges the notion of a singular, ideal trajectory for technological evolution. Instead, technologies emerge as solutions crafted for specific problems, tailored to specific users, within distinct temporal and spatial contexts.

The crux lies in how certain problems are defined as requiring solutions, which users are prioritized in the design process, what considerations are given to other technological systems, and who holds the authority to establish technical and economic priorities. These are fundamentally social considerations that intricately mold the trajectory of technological development. In essence, the evolution of technology is a dynamic interplay between technical functionalities and the social forces that navigate the complex terrain of problem-solving, user needs, and overarching societal priorities.

Just about every theatrical film throughout history (with some notable exceptions) is made up of many different pieces of film joined together into a mosaic of images. This is essentially the basis behind the "cut." A cut is an abrupt, but usually trivial film transition from one sequence to another.

Cuts are used in film to bring us from one image to the next. Unlike dissolves, cuts are instantaneous transitions; so as to say they don't occur gradually. A cut represents a total and instantaneous displacement of one field of vision with another.

The truth of the matter is that film is actually being "cut" twenty-four times a second. Each frame is a displacement from the previous one - it is just that in a continuous shot, the space/time displacement from frame to frame is small enough (twenty milliseconds) for the audience to see it as motion within a context rather than as twenty-four different contexts a second.

On the other hand, when the visual displacement is great enough (as at the moment of the cut), we are forced to re-evaluate the new image as a different context: miraculously, most of the time we have no problem in doing this.

What we do seem to have difficulty accepting are the kind of displacements that are neither subtle nor total: Cutting from a full-figure master shot, for instance, to a slightly tighter shot that frames the actors from the ankles up. The new shot in this case is different enough to signal that something has changed, but not different enough to make us re-evaluate its context: The displacement of the image is neither motion nor change of context, and the collision of these two ideas produces a mental jarring - a jump - that is comparatively disturbing.
